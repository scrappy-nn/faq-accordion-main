FAQs

What is dark matter?

Dark matter is an invisible substance that makes up about 27% of the universe. It doesn't emit or absorb light, but its gravitational effects are seen in the behavior of galaxies and galaxy clusters. Understanding dark matter helps explain the structure and evolution of the universe.

How do black holes form?

Black holes form when massive stars collapse under their own gravity at the end of their life cycle. This collapse creates a singularity with an event horizon, beyond which nothing can escape. Black holes are characterized by their mass, spin, and sometimes charge.

What is the cosmic microwave background (CMB)?

The CMB is the remnant radiation from the Big Bang, visible as a faint glow across the entire sky. It provides crucial evidence for the Big Bang theory and helps scientists understand the early universe's conditions and the fundamental parameters of cosmology.

What are exoplanets and why are they important?

Exoplanets are planets orbiting stars outside our solar system. They vary widely in size, composition, and conditions. Studying exoplanets helps us understand planetary systems' formation and evolution and aids in the search for potentially habitable worlds and extraterrestrial life.
